Project Overview

The modern concept of "homosexuals" as a distinct group is not an eternal category but a historical construct. This digital archive traces profound ruptures in Western cultural understandings of same-sex desire, contrasting the philosophical frameworks of classical Athens with the medicalized paradigms of late 19th-century Europe.

By juxtaposing these two distant historical moments, this collection challenges the notion of a continuous, transhistorical "homosexuality", revealing instead how our contemporary categories of sexual orientation are contingent constructs shaped by specific cultural, scientific, and political forces.

The Central Argument

Michel Foucault posited that the late 19th century marked the "birth" of modern homosexuality, noting that what was once viewed as the crime of sodomy‚Äîa prohibited act anyone could potentially commit‚Äîhad transformed into homosexuality, a psychological state revealing an individual's deepest identity.

This archive makes this abstract theoretical claim tangible through visual and textual evidence. Greek vase paintings depict courtship scenes and symposium imagery, celebrating same-sex eros as an integrated aspect of civic and intellectual life within visual culture, free from the stigma of abnormality. In contrast, Victorian medical illustrations, Freud's psychosexual development diagrams, and sexological case studies present same-sex desire as a condition to be diagnosed, explained, and controlled by expert knowledge.

The archive ultimately argues that our contemporary categories of sexual orientation‚Äîhomosexual, heterosexual, bisexual, pansexual‚Äîare not discoveries of natural facts but historical inventions created through specific regimes of power and knowledge.
