The Platonic Era: Eros as Act and Philosophy

In ancient Athens, same-sex desire between men was understood primarily through frameworks of ethics, pedagogy, and social hierarchy. The Symposium presents eros between men as a vehicle for philosophical enlightenment, a force that could elevate the soul from physical beauty to contemplation of absolute Beauty itself.

These relationships, particularly the culturally sanctioned practice of pederasty between adult men (erastai) and adolescent youths (eromenoi), were evaluated based on their adherence to social conventions, their role in transmitting civic virtue, and their conformity to ideals of moderation and reciprocity.

The ancient Greeks had no concept of "the homosexual" as a type of person; they recognized only acts that could be performed by any citizen, acts that were judged by their context, propriety, and social function.

Visual and Textual Sources

Red-Figure Kylix with Courtship Scene

Greek Pottery | c. 460 BCE | Boston Museum of Fine Arts, Acc. No. 10.181

This red-figure kylix from the Boston Museum of Fine Arts depicts two male figures in an intimate exchange that exemplifies the visual conventions of Greek pederastic courtship. A bearded older man (erastes) extends his right hand toward a younger, beardless male (eromenos), offering what appears to be a wreath or decorative chain—a typical gift meant to honor the beloved and demonstrate the suitor's worthiness. The youth stands in a respectful, receptive posture, embodying the cultural expectation of measured response rather than eager acceptance.

Kenneth Dover's analysis of such pottery reveals that these courtship scenes followed strict social codes governing appropriate pursuit and response. The erastes demonstrates his virtue through honorable gifts and restrained approach, while the eromenos maintains his dignity through modest reception. Significantly, this scene decorates a drinking vessel used at symposia—elite male gatherings where such relationships were openly discussed and celebrated. The public display of this imagery on symposium ware demonstrates that these relationships were integrated into civic life rather than hidden or pathologized. This vase perfectly illustrates the Greek paradigm: same-sex desire was evaluated as socially regulated behavior judged by propriety and context, not as an identity category defining a type of person.

Red-Figure Kylix Depicting Scenes from a Symposium

Greek Pottery | c. 480 BCE | Boston Museum of Fine Arts, Acc. No. 01.8034

This red-figure kylix from the Boston Museum of Fine Arts captures the intimate atmosphere of an Athenian symposium, depicting two men reclining together on a kline (dining couch) in the characteristic posture of symposiasts. One bearded figure holds a lyre, preparing to play or having just played music, while engaging closely with his companion. The scene illustrates how music, poetry, intellectual discourse, and erotic attraction intermingled at these elite male gatherings. The physical proximity of the reclining figures and their engaged interaction demonstrate the accepted intimacy of these occasions.

The symposium was the central setting for Plato's famous dialogue on Eros, and this visual evidence confirms the textual accounts: these were spaces where relationships between men flourished openly. Dover emphasizes that symposium vessels like this kylix were not merely decorative but reflected actual social practices, used at the very gatherings they depicted. The scene demonstrates same-sex eros as contextual behavior embedded in specific institutional settings—the elite drinking party where knowledge was transmitted, political alliances formed, and erotic bonds cultivated. Unlike the modern concept of homosexuality as a fixed personal identity, the Greeks understood these interactions as situational behaviors proper to certain social contexts, evaluated by their adherence to cultural norms of moderation and reciprocity rather than by the gender of the participants.

Roman Warren Cup

Artifact | c. 15 BC | British Museum, Museum No. 1999,0426.1

The Warren Cup depicts explicit male-male intercourse rendered with remarkable technical refinement. Unlike Greek pederastic courtship scenes, the Roman imagery eroticizes adult male bodies and presents intimacy without pedagogical framing or moral commentary. The scenes illustrate continuity of same-sex acts across antiquity while highlighting the absence of identity-based categories: Roman culture did not classify participants as deviant, but integrated such imagery into elite dining and social display.

This object contrasts sharply with philosophical texts like Plato’s Symposium. Whereas Plato moralizes desire by directing it toward virtue and the Forms, the Warren Cup treats erotic acts as aesthetic, pleasurable, and socially embedded. Its presence demonstrates that the ancient world acknowledged same-sex acts without producing the stable sexual identities codified by modern sexology.

Key Takeaways from the Platonic Era

Acts, Not Identities: Ancient Greeks evaluated same-sex relationships based on conduct, social appropriateness, and philosophical value—not as expressions of an innate sexual identity.

Public Celebration: Same-sex desire was depicted openly on drinking vessels and discussed in philosophical dialogues, integrated into civic and intellectual life rather than pathologized or hidden.

Pedagogical Function: Pederastic relationships served as vehicles for transmitting knowledge, virtue, and wisdom from one generation to the next.

Philosophical Elevation: Plato's Symposium presents eros between men as the beginning of philosophical ascent toward transcendent truth and beauty.
