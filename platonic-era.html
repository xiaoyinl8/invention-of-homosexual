The Platonic Era: Eros as Act and Philosophy

In ancient Athens, same-sex desire between men was understood primarily through frameworks of ethics, pedagogy, and social hierarchy. The Symposium presents eros between men as a vehicle for philosophical enlightenment, a force that could elevate the soul from physical beauty to contemplation of absolute Beauty itself.

These relationships, particularly the culturally sanctioned practice of pederasty between adult men (erastai) and adolescent youths (eromenoi), were evaluated based on their adherence to social conventions, their role in transmitting civic virtue, and their conformity to ideals of moderation and reciprocity.

The ancient Greeks had no concept of "the homosexual" as a type of person; they recognized only acts that could be performed by any citizen, acts that were judged by their context, propriety, and social function.

Visual and Textual Sources

Red-Figure Kylix with Courtship Scene

Greek Pottery | c. 460 BCE | Boston Museum of Fine Arts, Acc. No. 10.181

This red-figure kylix depicts a classic scene of Greek pederastic courtship. A bearded, older man (the erastes) offers a gift to a younger, beardless youth (the eromenos), a visual convention that follows strict social codes. The suitor demonstrates his honorable intentions through the gift, while the beloved maintains his dignity through a reserved posture.

Crucially, this intimate scene decorates a drinking cup used at symposia, indicating these relationships were openly celebrated within elite male social life. As Kenneth Dover’s analysis confirms, such imagery shows that same-sex desire was framed through social roles and regulated behavior. It was judged by its context and propriety, not as an identity that defined a person’s fundamental nature. This was an accepted social practice, not a category of being.

Red-Figure Kylix Depicting Scenes from a Symposium

Greek Pottery | c. 480 BCE | Boston Museum of Fine Arts, Acc. No. 01.8034

This red-figure kylix from the Boston Museum of Fine Arts captures the intimate symposium scene, depicting two men reclining together on a dining couch in the characteristic posture of symposiasts. The scene illustrates how music, poetry, intellectual discourse, and erotic attraction intermixed at these elite male gatherings.

The symposium was the central setting for Plato's famous dialogue on Eros, and this visual evidence confirms the textual accounts: these were spaces where relationships between men flourished openly. Dover emphasizes that symposium vessels like this kylix were not merely decorative but reflected actual social practices, used at the very gatherings they depicted. The scene demonstrates same-sex eros as contextual behavior embedded in specific institutional settings—the elite drinking party where knowledge was transmitted, political alliances formed, and erotic bonds cultivated.

Roman Warren Cup

Artifact | c. 15 BC | British Museum, Museum No. 1999,0426.1

The Warren Cup depicts explicit male-male intercourse rendered with remarkable technical refinement. Unlike Greek pederastic courtship scenes, the Roman imagery eroticizes adult male bodies and presents intimacy without pedagogical framing or moral commentary. The scenes illustrate continuity of same-sex acts across antiquity while highlighting the absence of identity-based categories: Roman culture did not classify participants as deviant, but integrated such imagery into elite dining and social display.

This object stands in sharp contrast to philosophical works like Plato’s Symposium, which sought to channel erotic desire toward transcendent virtue. Instead, the Warren Cup frames same-sex acts simply as aspects of pleasure and daily experience. Its very existence underscores a key distinction: classical antiquity acknowledged a wide spectrum of sexual behaviors without categorizing individuals into fixed identities, a conceptual framework that would only emerge centuries later.

Plato's Symposium

Speech of Pausanias

4th century BCE

Excerpt from Plato's Symposium (Pausanias's Speech)

Classical Text | 4th century BCE

In his speech from Plato’s Symposium, Pausanias distinguishes between a “Common Love,” focused on physical pleasure, and a “Heavenly Love,” which directs desire toward virtue and intellectual growth. He argues that relationships between men are superior when they foster moral and civic excellence.

This framework evaluates love solely by its ethical purpose and the conduct it inspires, not by the gender of the participants. The beloved’s gender is secondary to the relationship’s capacity to guide the soul toward the good. This passage exemplifies a classical worldview that had no concept of sexual orientation. It provides a direct philosophical contrast to the 19th-century model, which would later anchor desire to an innate, identity-defining condition rather than to ethical striving.

Plato's Laws

4th century BCE

Excerpt from Plato’s Laws, Book 8

Classical Text | 4th century BCE

In Laws, Plato condemns physical intimacy between men as “contrary to nature” and a failure of self-mastery that should be outlawed. This legalistic perspective starkly contrasts with the philosophical praise found in his Symposium.

However, even in this censure, the focus remains on governing specific acts deemed detrimental to the city’s social and procreative order. Plato frames the issue as one of vice and unlawful behavior, not as a symptom of a distinct type of person. This highlights a crucial distinction: classical thought, whether celebrating or condemning, evaluated sexual conduct through ethical and civic lenses. It operated without the modern concept of a fixed sexual identity, which would later redefine such desires as the core of a person’s being.

Greek Homosexuality

Kenneth J. Dover

1978

Kenneth Dover's Greek Homosexuality: Scholarly Analysis

Historical Analysis | 1978

Kenneth Dover’s analysis reveals that Greek same-sex relations were structured by social conventions, not personal identity. His study of art and literature shows that the culturally sanctioned practice of pederasty followed a strict code of conduct between the older erastes (lover) and the younger eromenos (beloved), governed by ideals of honor and civic virtue.

Dover confirms that the Greeks had no concept of a fixed sexual orientation. A man’s participation in these relationships was a temporary social role, entirely compatible with later marriage and fatherhood. These were context-dependent behaviors judged by their adherence to social norms, not expressions of an innate identity. His work thus provides critical evidence that same-sex acts pre-dated the modern invention of “the homosexual” as a distinct category of person.

Key Takeaways from the Platonic Era

Acts, Not Identities: Ancient Greeks evaluated same-sex relationships based on conduct, social appropriateness, and philosophical value—not as expressions of an innate sexual identity.

Public Celebration: Same-sex desire was depicted openly on drinking vessels and discussed in philosophical dialogues, integrated into civic and intellectual life rather than pathologized or hidden.

Pedagogical Function: Pederastic relationships served as vehicles for transmitting knowledge, virtue, and wisdom from one generation to the next.

Philosophical Elevation: Plato's Symposium presents eros between men as the beginning of philosophical ascent toward transcendent truth and beauty.
